# cited-skincare

An evidence-based skincare and haircare routine reference for Indian skin and hair types, climate, and product availability — with every claim traceable to a source.

Built because most skincare content online is brand marketing wearing a science costume. This isn't that. Every recommendation links to a peer-reviewed study, a clinical trial, or a sourced community thread — tap the numbered tag next to any claim to see exactly where it came from.

**[→ View the Live Guide](https://mrigank590.github.io/cited-skincare)**

## What's inside

- **4 face-skin types** (oily/acne-prone, dry, combination, sensitive) — each with a morning/night routine, a common-mistake warning, and real product picks used in India
- **4 hair/scalp types** (oily + dandruff, dry/frizzy, hair fall, curly/wavy) — same structure
- **A dedicated "Red flags" tab** — six things worth knowing before you spend money: misleading ad claims, fake ingredient percentages, counterfeit risk, chemical sunscreen filter concerns, the hair-oil/dandruff link, and the most common self-inflicted routine mistake
- **32 cited sources** — dermatology journals (IJDVL), peer-reviewed studies (PubMed-indexed), Indian clinical trials, regulatory enforcement data (ASCI), and cross-referenced Reddit communities (r/IndianSkincareAddicts, r/curlyhair)

## Why this exists

Three patterns kept showing up in the research that most routine guides quietly skip:

1. **Indian sunlight is 95–98% UVA**, not UVB — so SPF alone is the wrong metric. PA rating matters just as much, and most generic skincare advice doesn't mention it.
2. **Brand claims in India are unreliable at scale.** ASCI flagged over 500 brands for misleading advertising in 2025–26 alone, including major D2C and FMCG names. "% niacinamide" on a label is frequently not what it claims to be — checking ingredient-list position takes ten seconds and catches most of it.
3. **More actives is the most common way people damage their own skin barrier.** Layering retinol, acids, and vitamin C at once is a near-universal beginner mistake that dermatologists report seeing constantly.

The citation system exists so none of this has to be taken on faith — you can check the source yourself, the same way you'd check a footnote in an actual paper.

## Sourcing notes

Confidence varies by section, and this README won't pretend otherwise:

- **Sunscreen/UV science**: strong — sourced from the Indian Journal of Dermatology, Venereology and Leprology (IJDVL) and a 230-subject Delhi clinical trial run specifically on Indian skin types IV–V.
- **Coconut oil mechanism**: strong — sourced from a PubMed-indexed peer-reviewed study (Rele & Mohile, *Journal of Cosmetic Science*, 2003, PMID 12715094).
- **Regulatory/counterfeit claims**: strong — sourced from ASCI's published 2025–26 enforcement data.
- **Specific product picks**: mixed. Sunscreens and cleansers are backed by genuine, checkable Reddit/review-aggregate sentiment. Hair-brand picks have thinner sourcing — where the evidence was weak, the recommendation was kept generic ("any pure coconut oil") rather than overstated.

## Disclaimer

This is a reference tool, not medical advice. It is not a substitute for a dermatologist, especially for diagnosed conditions (androgenetic alopecia, eczema, diagnosed acne, etc.). Where the content says "see a dermatologist," it means it.
